Sam & the Guides: A System of Coordinated Low-Cost Robots for Indoor Guidance of Blind and Visually-Impaired People

Market, technology, competitive and impact assessment — compiled from the research team's findings. Prepared 17 July 2026.

1. Executive Summary

"Sam & the Guides" is a concept for a camera-free, internet-free system of three low-cost robots and a coordinating phone app that physically leads a blind or visually-impaired person along pre-mapped indoor routes. This report assesses the opportunity across demographics, market size, competition, incumbent alternatives, technical feasibility, academic precedent, regulation, business model, risk and social impact, drawing only on the team's sourced findings.

  • The need is large, growing and under-served. At least 2.2 billion people worldwide have a vision impairment [1]; an estimated 43 million were blind in 2020, projected to reach about 61 million by 2050 as populations age [2]. Yet only 28% of blind and partially sighted people feel able to travel independently, and unfamiliar indoor spaces — the exact target venues — defeat the white cane because they lack tactile landmarks [3][4].
  • The incumbent "physical guidance" solution does not scale. Guide dogs cost roughly US$45,000–$60,000 to train [27], serve only about 2% of blind people [28], and number fewer than 19,000 working animals worldwide — a total that is actually declining [29]. The white cane, used by the majority, only senses obstacles by contact below the waist within ~1.2–1.5 m and misses head-height hazards, which injure over 40% of users monthly [34][35].
  • The enabling technology is mature and cheap. Ultra-wideband radios give 10–30 cm follow-the-leader precision [36], BLE beacons cost a few dollars each [37], ultrasonic sensors cost $1–3 [38], commercial "follow-me" modules already exist [39], and camera-free operation sidesteps real privacy/HIPAA barriers in care homes and hospitals [42][71].
  • The market is real and regulation is a tailwind. The assistive-technology-for-visually-impaired market is roughly $6.3B in 2025, growing ~12% a year [9], accelerated by the European Accessibility Act (enforceable June 2025) and a web of accessibility mandates on the exact institutional buyers targeted [52][53].
  • The closest commercial competitor validates the model but is different. Glidance's "Glide" ($1,499 hardware + ~$30/month) physically steers one user via a hand-held handle, but was not yet shipping in mid-2026 [17][48]. No shipping product uses a coordinated multi-robot escort — a genuine novelty opportunity and, equally, an unproven-architecture risk.

Bottom line: The problem, the demographics, the enabling technology, the pricing headroom and the regulatory environment all support a low-cost physical-guidance system for institutions. The principal uncertainties are architectural (no multi-robot precedent), operational (dense dynamic crowds remain unsolved even for the field's most advanced project [46]) and behavioural (assistive devices are abandoned at high rates without co-design [70]).

2. The Problem & Who It Affects

Vision impairment is one of the largest health conditions globally. At least 2.2 billion people have a near or distance vision impairment, and for at least 1 billion of them the condition is preventable or still unaddressed; the annual global productivity cost is estimated at US$411 billion [1]. An estimated 43.3 million people were blind and 295 million had moderate-to-severe vision impairment as of 2020, and most people with vision impairment are over the age of 50 [1] — a demographic skew that maps directly onto the care-home and hospital settings the product targets.

The population is expanding, not shrinking. By 2050 the number of blind people is projected to rise to about 61 million and those with moderate/severe impairment to 474 million; between 1990 and 2020 the count of blind adults aged 50+ grew 50.6% even as age-standardised rates fell 28.5%, because population growth and ageing outpace medical progress [2]. In the U.S. alone, roughly 60% of people with vision impairment are 65 or older, 1 in 4 adults over 71 are affected, and total cases are expected to double to more than 8 million by 2050 [7].

Indoor mobility is the sharpest unmet need

RNIB research found only 28% of blind and partially sighted people feel able to travel independently, with just 42% confident moving around their own home and 43% outdoors — even after receiving support [3]. Indoor navigation is uniquely hard: unlike outdoors, blind people cannot use curbs, walls and traffic sound as landmarks, which makes first-time navigation of an unfamiliar building — airports, stations, museums — effectively "too difficult to attempt" [4]. Today the fallback in complex public buildings is scarce, on-demand human assistance: U.S. airlines are legally required to escort blind passengers, but the passenger must self-identify and request it, and terminal wayfinding is described as a major challenge affecting mobility and quality of life for the 4M+ Americans with impaired vision [8].

The downstream harms

The mobility gap tracks directly with severe secondary harms. Only about 44% of people who are blind or visually impaired are employed, versus 79% of people without disabilities, and over half of working-age blind/VI adults are entirely out of the labour force [5]. Meta-analytic evidence puts diagnosed depression among blind people at about 25%, with significantly higher rates of anxiety and social isolation than the sighted population — with loss of independent mobility repeatedly cited as a driver [6]. Restoring independent movement is therefore a mental-health and economic-inclusion lever, not merely a convenience.

The overwhelming majority of blind-navigation products are audio-only — smartphone apps or an open standard that announce turn-by-turn directions over BLE beacons, coded tags or LiDAR, but leave the user to walk unaided with a cane. Costs are borne almost entirely by charities, and a dog that washes out mid-programme still represents a loss of more than $12,000 [27]. Roughly 65–77% of failures are behavioural rather than health-related [31]. The result is a tiny, shrinking, expensive-to-replenish pool against a blind population in the tens of millions — a supply wall the incumbent model cannot scale past.

The white cane: cheap but fundamentally limited

The white cane, used by the overwhelming majority, detects hazards only by physical contact, below waist level, within a reach of just ~1.2–1.5 m — giving no advance warning and no ability to detect head-height or overhead obstacles [34]. It also cannot tell the user where a destination is or route them there; it only reveals what the tip already touches [34]. In a survey of 300 visually impaired people, over 40% reported head-level accidents at least once a month, and 23% of those incidents had medical consequences [35]. This is precisely the failure mode a mapped, actively-guiding system can address.

6. Technology & Feasibility (Why Camera-Free / Low-Cost Works)

The camera-free indoor-guidance stack the concept depends on is mature, cheap and — importantly — commercially off-the-shelf.

  • Precise robot-to-robot spacing (UWB). Ultra-wideband delivers 10–30 cm indoor positioning via true time-of-flight, versus 3–5 m for signal-strength BLE, with high multipath resistance in complex spaces like hospitals and airports — precise enough to hold fixed spacing and speed without a camera [36].
  • Cheap zone localization (BLE beacons). BLE beacons cost roughly $5–$35 each, run 2–8 years on a coin cell, and use existing phones/tablets as readers, cutting deployment cost by ~60%. RSSI gives ~3–5 m zone accuracy; Bluetooth 5.1 Angle-of-Arrival tightens this to 0.3–0.5 m — enough to place each robot in the correct corridor along a pre-mapped, no-internet route [37].
  • Obstacle/spacing sensing (ultrasonic). The HC-SR04 sensor measures 2 cm–400 cm at ~±3 mm within a 30° cone, works regardless of lighting or matte-black surfaces, and costs ~$1–3 in bulk [38].
  • The follow primitive already ships. Commercial UWB "follow-me" anchor/tag modules (e.g. Weston Robot) let a robot track and follow a person or another robot to within a few centimetres at ranges up to 50 m — essentially the exact primitive the concept needs [39].
  • Proven control theory. Leader-follower is the foundational multi-robot formation-control method, valued because it is "simple and easy to implement," but it assumes reliable localization and high-frequency communication [40] — which is exactly why fixed pre-mapped routes plus UWB/BLE anchors matter.

The key technical risk: drift

Pedestrian/inertial dead-reckoning from smartphone MEMS IMUs suffers error that accumulates with time, so accurate systems must correct it with short-range radio anchors or map-matching rather than IMU alone [41]. The peer-reviewed consensus is that hybrid designs pairing dead-reckoning with BLE/UWB fixed points and pre-mapped route matching produce a "drift-free" path — validating the beacon-anchored, pre-mapped-route architecture and flagging IMU-only navigation as unsafe [41].

Camera-free is a compliance advantage, not just a cost choice

Video surveillance faces explicit privacy, dignity and HIPAA barriers in the exact target venues; guidance for care homes tells operators to weigh "whether less privacy intrusive solutions could achieve the same objectives" [42]. A camera-free guidance robot avoids this entire consent/compliance burden — a concrete adoption advantage over camera-based guide robots for institutional buyers.

Cost feasibility and a cheap fallback

Guide-dog scarcity and cost are the market gap, and researchers estimate a robotic guide could fall below $2,000 [26]; peer-reviewed work (RDog, CHI 2024) shows physical guidance plus voice cues produces faster, smoother navigation with fewer collisions and lower cognitive load for blind users [43]. For fixed corridors, magnetic-tape/line-following AGV guidance is "often more cost-effective than other types of AGVs," and line-following robot kits sell for $150–$300 — a proven, low-cost routing fallback that complements radio positioning at turns and stops [44].

7. Academic & Industry Precedents

The precedent base is deep and credible — and carries two hard lessons.

  • CMU/IBM "AI Suitcase" (CaBot). Led by blind IBM Fellow Chieko Asakawa since 2017, it has run field trials in a shopping mall, museum and airport involving ~2,200 participants (about a quarter visually impaired) as of Sept 2024; in the original 2019 study, blind testers rated it favourably against a guide dog or sighted guide [45]. It entered permanent daily operation at Japan's Miraikan museum in April 2024, and 83% of blind participants were "very satisfied" or "satisfied" [73]. It is now backed by a four-company consortium (Alps Alpine, OMRON, Shimizu, IBM Japan) and demonstrated daily at Expo 2025 Osaka [47].
  • The unsolved-problem flag. Despite ~8 years of development, the AI Suitcase has not been commercialized, blocked by outdoor position measurement and complex/dynamic obstacles such as crowds [46] — the very conditions in the airports and stations the concept targets. Its latest model still relies on a built-in camera and generative AI [47], the opposite of the no-camera/no-internet premise.
  • Academic quadrupeds. RDog (CHI 2024, Unitree Go1 EDU with five fisheye stereo-camera sets plus ultrasonics) measurably outperformed cane baselines on speed, collisions and cognitive load [43]. Binghamton University fitted a Unitree Go2 with GPT-4 and tested it with 7 legally blind participants [50]; the University of Glasgow's "RoboGuide" partnered with RNIB Scotland and was demonstrated in 2024 [51]. These validate the physical-guidance thesis but depend on multi-camera perception and involve tiny sample sizes (often <10 users), i.e. proof-of-concept, not clinical validation.
  • Hardware cost benchmark. The dominant research platform, Unitree's Go2, sells for ~$1,600 (Air) to $2,800 (Pro), with research-grade EDU units quote-only at roughly $3,800–$16,000+ [49]. Off-the-shelf legged platforms are already near the target price, implying the concept's differentiation must come from multi-robot coordination and the app, not raw hardware cost.

Net. Autonomous physical guidance of blind users indoors is demonstrably achievable and increasingly funded by major industrial players. But every technically successful system leans on cameras, LiDAR and heavy compute; the field's most mature project is still stuck on dense dynamic crowds; and the only shipping consumer product is a single self-guided device [17][48], not a multi-robot swarm — so the three-robot architecture is both novel and unproven.

9. Business Model & Pricing

Pricing anchors exist on every side. The incumbent (a guide dog) costs $45,000–$60,000 to produce but is charity-funded and free to the user [27], leaving enormous cost-advantage headroom. The closest robot comparable validates a hardware-plus-SaaS split. And — most relevant for the stated institutional buyers — the strongest precedent is venue-sponsored / per-site models, not consumer sales.

Recommended structure

  • Lead with per-site institutional sale/lease. Aira and GoodMaps are already paid for by institutions and free to blind end-users [62][63] — the template for airports, hospitals and museums. At RaaS rates of ~$450–$1,200/robot/month [64], a full three-robot kit could plausibly lease for ~$1,350–$3,600/month per site before software, converting capex into recurring revenue and lowering the adoption barrier.
  • Optional D2C tier. If sold to individuals, follow the validated hardware-plus-SaaS split: a low device price (~$1,000–$1,500) plus a modest subscription — Glidance sets the high end at ~$30/mo [60] and WeWALK the low end at ~$4/mo [61].
  • Lean on existing funding rails. U.S. assistive tech for blind/low-vision users is routinely funded through Vocational Rehabilitation, VA services (VIST/BROS) and community grants such as Lions Clubs — rarely paid fully out of pocket [67], supporting a "get funded, then buy" motion. The WHO/UNICEF Global Report on Assistive Technology found affordability is the top barrier and that access can be as low as 3% of need in low-income settings [66] — reinforcing that low-cost, offline design and subsidized/institutional pricing address the market's core constraint.

10. Risks, Ethics & Safety

  • Safety certification is non-negotiable. A robot that physically leads a human is a "personal care robot" under ISO 13482:2014 (currently being revised), which mandates hazard identification, protective stops, and speed/force limits. Certification against it is effectively the entry ticket for selling into hospitals, care homes and airports [68].
  • Liability is legally unsettled. Fault for an autonomous-robot accident may lie with the manufacturer, programmer, integrator or operator; an EU Parliament proposal (2017) for a manufacturer-funded compensation fund was never enacted [69]. For a device guiding a vulnerable user, contracts and insurance must allocate this explicitly before deployment — buyers' insurers will probe it.
  • Abandonment is the dominant adoption risk — and it is human, not technical. Roughly 29.3% of assistive devices are abandoned outright, with 25–75% abandoned within three years, and mobility aids abandoned most often [70]. The predictors are lack of user involvement in selection, poor performance, changing needs and undiscriminating procurement [70] — making co-design with blind users, demonstrable reliability and a non-stigmatizing form factor core requirements, not marketing.
  • Trust is measurable and fragile. HRI research on guide robots formally measured user trust and found blind users demand explainability, gradual turns, semantic communication and a rigid handle before relying on the robot; trust collapses on jerky motion or silent failures [43]. The voice-direction channel and predictable spacing/speed control are therefore trust-and-safety features that determine whether the device is used at all.
  • Camera-free is an ethical and compliance asset. Both blind users and sighted bystanders share documented privacy concerns about camera-based aids [71]. A no-camera, no-internet design removes the bystander-surveillance objection and cloud-breach surface entirely, and materially simplifies GDPR / EU AI Act obligations — the AI Act bans systems that exploit disability-based vulnerabilities and treats biometric analytics as high-risk, triggers a camera-free design sidesteps [72].
  • Distributive-justice risk. 90%+ of vision loss is in low/middle-income regions [1], so a genuinely low-cost offline device has an equity advantage — but pricing and support that reach only wealthy institutions would undercut the product's stated social mission.

11. Go-to-Market Strategy

The strongest beachhead is high-footfall institutions that already procure accessibility infrastructure and are legally motivated to do so. Adoption should follow proven B2B/B2B2C accessibility precedents (Aira, GoodMaps) rather than consumer retail.

Beachhead sequencing

  • Airports first (best-proven). In 2024, 5.5 million passengers (~1.9%) requested special assistance at UK airports, up from 0.9% in 2010, and 75% of airports now use digital tools to coordinate assistance [74] — rising volumes straining human assistants, a direct labour-augmentation pitch. GoodMaps' indoor-navigation platform is now live in 35 airports across 8 countries, having quadrupled its footprint since January 2025 [75] — direct proof that airports procure and deploy blind-navigation tech at scale and speed.
  • Care homes (concentrated, repeatable). The UK alone has ~16,566 care homes with ~441,000 residents and an estimated 144,000 additional beds needed over the next decade [80]; fixed indoor routes (room–dining room–garden), a captive vision-impaired-and-elderly population, and central procurement favour per-site fleet sales.
  • Museums & universities (reference-able lighthouses). Institutions already buy indoor navigation for blind visitors: the University of Guelph deployed ~400 BlindSquare points campus-wide, the NC Museum of Natural Sciences installed 30 Bluetooth beacons, and the Smithsonian offers Aira free across its DC museums [77] — proven secondary beachheads with accessibility budgets and mission-driven procurement rationale.

The institutional ROI narrative

Analogous B2B robot adoption already justifies autonomous mobile robots on staff-time-saved grounds: professional service-robot sales reached ~200,000 units in 2024 (+9%), the hospital delivery/logistics robot market was ~US$9.4B in 2024, hospitals see ROI in 12–18 months, and Diligent Robotics' Moxi frees nurses ~30 minutes per shift [79]. The same logic — freeing PRM assistants and care staff — anchors the guiding-robot pitch. Partnerships with disability charities (e.g. RNIB, already engaged with the Glasgow RoboGuide project [51]) provide a credible channel into the care-home/university buyers.

12. Social Impact

The unmet need is the core impact story: more than 2.5 billion people need at least one assistive product, yet nearly 1 billion are denied access — a figure rising to 3.5 billion by 2050, with access as low as 3% of need in some low/middle-income countries [66]. A low-cost, camera-free, internet-free device targeting institutions is designed precisely around affordability, the most-cited barrier [66].

The impact can be quantified with accepted methods. Disability-independence and eye-care interventions are commonly evaluated with Social Return on Investment (SROI): a Deloitte study found every $1 invested in community eye care generated $7.30 in social value in India and $5.02 in Nepal, while a visually-impaired education project returned RM4.48 per RM1 [78] — establishing a ~$4–7 social-value-per-$1 benchmark a grant or impact-investor pitch can use to value restored independence, employment and wellbeing. The downstream harms the product targets are large and measurable: ~44% employment versus 79% [5], ~25% depression prevalence [6], and pronounced social isolation [6]. Restoring independent indoor mobility is therefore a lever on employment, mental health and dignity — and, deployed equitably, on global access rather than only wealthy-institution convenience.

13. Conclusion

The evidence supports a clear thesis: there is a large, ageing, under-served population [1][2]; independent indoor mobility is its sharpest unmet need [3][4]; the incumbent physical-guidance solution (guide dogs) is structurally unable to scale [27][28][29]; the white cane cannot route or protect the user above the waist [34][35]; the enabling camera-free technology is mature, cheap and partly off-the-shelf [36][37][38][39]; the market is real and growing under a strengthening regulatory tailwind [9][52]; and validated business models exist for selling accessibility navigation to the exact institutional buyers targeted [62][63].

Three risks temper the opportunity and should shape the roadmap. First, architecture: no shipping product uses a coordinated multi-robot escort, so the three-robot design is genuinely novel and genuinely unproven [17][48]. Second, operations: even the field's most advanced project remains uncommercialized after eight years because of dense, dynamic crowds — the target environments [46]. Third, human factors: assistive devices are abandoned at high rates absent co-design, reliability and trust [70][43]. A disciplined path — camera-free by design for compliance advantage [42][71][72], safety-certified to ISO 13482 [68], sold per-site to mandate-driven institutions with an ROI narrative [64][79], and co-designed with blind users from day one [70] — directly addresses each. On balance, "Sam & the Guides" occupies a real and defensible gap: low-cost physical guidance, camera-free, for institutions that are increasingly obliged to provide it.
