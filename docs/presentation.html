A camera-free swarm of three robots that listens, coordinates, and physically guides a blind traveler through a mapped city — using nothing but spoken words.

The problem

Getting somewhere new is still the hard part.

250M+people worldwide are blind or have low vision.
The white cane

Feels the ground — not the way.

It finds the next step and the next obstacle, but it can't tell you which way leads to the door.

The guide dog

Costs a small fortune.

Tens of thousands of dollars and up to two years of training — and it can't learn a brand-new building on demand.

The gap: on-demand guidance through unfamiliar spaces — an airport, a campus, a station you've never walked before.

Our idea

What if a small team of robots
could lead you there — just by asking?

You speak a destination. The system understands it, plans the route, and a formation of robots physically walks you to it — narrating every step out loud.

No camera

Nothing to film the people around you. Privacy by design.

No screen to read

The interface is your voice and a calm guiding voice back.

Works as a team

Three robots share one job: keeping you on the path, together.

The hard part

A swarm is only as good as the command
that reaches all of it.

Wireless commands get lost, delayed, or arrive out of order. So we rebuilt the whole communication layer to be reliable, deterministic, and verifiable.

  • Every command is acknowledged by each robot — the hub knows exactly who received it.
  • Lost commands are retried automatically; the fleet starts in sync.
  • The system verifies all three are actually moving — or it stops everyone for safety.
  • Survives dropouts with automatic reconnection, and shows each robot's state live.
✓ Tested at 40% simulated packet loss → still 3/3 delivered
The mapped city

We built a miniature town — and handed the robots its map.

Every street measured to the centimeter. The hub turns a destination into an exact sequence of distances and turns, so the swarm navigates by dead-reckoning — no lines to follow, no camera to see.

What we learned

The motors were never the hard part.

The real problem

Wireless reliability

Coordinating three robots over Bluetooth — not driving them — is where a swarm succeeds or fails.

Our response

Rebuild for trust

We rearchitected communication around acknowledgment and verification, then stress-tested it against heavy packet loss.

Engineering lesson

Robust beats fast

A command that always arrives — or safely aborts — is worth more than one that's quick but uncertain.

Design lesson

Simplicity scales

Keeping the robots "dumb" and the hub smart made the whole system cheaper, clearer, and easier to trust.
